What is HCI? (Chapter 1, pp. 11–20)

Human–Computer Interaction (HCI) studies how humans interact with computers and how systems can be designed to be more usable. It emphasizes design, evaluation, and implementation of interactive systems.

Cognition and Human Information Processing (pp. 15–20)

Human cognition involves perception, attention, processing, and action. Designers should account for cognitive limits and mental workload.

  • Perception: Sensing input from the interface
  • Attention: Selecting relevant information
  • Processing: Interpreting and reasoning with information
  • Decision & Action: Executing a response or interaction

Short-Term Memory and Chunking (pp. 26–30)

Short-term memory can hold 7 ± 2 items. Grouping related items into chunks reduces memory load.

Fitts’ Law: Size and Distance (pp. 20–25)

Selection time depends on the size and distance of targets. Important controls should be large and easy to reach.

Mental Models and User Expectations (pp. 30–35)

Users form mental models of how systems work. Aligning system behavior with these models reduces errors and frustration.
